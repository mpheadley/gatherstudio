## The Core Problem

AI-generated copy is sanded smooth. It is correct, balanced, and empty. The person reading it has no idea if a human being actually believes what they're reading. That's why it doesn't convert. The fix is not style — it's specificity and honesty.

---

## The AI Kill List

These words appear so often in AI-generated copy that they function as tells. Cut them. All of them.

**Verbs:**
delve, foster, leverage, harness, underscore, highlight, showcase, navigate, bolster, streamline, optimize, unlock, unleash, elevate, align, enhance, empower, revolutionize, supercharge, transform

**Adjectives and adverbs:**
robust, comprehensive, pivotal, crucial, vital, seamless, dynamic, nuanced, multifaceted, innovative, cutting-edge, unprecedented, vibrant, intricate, fundamentally, notably

**Nouns and compound nouns:**
synergy, paradigm, ecosystem, landscape, bandwidth, deliverables, stakeholders, touchpoints

**Phrases:**
- "It's worth noting that..."
- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "This plays a significant role in shaping..."
- "At the end of the day..."
- "The intersection of X and Y"
- "A tapestry of..."
- "From X to Y, ..."
- "Whether you're a...or a..."
- "This is not just about X, it's about Y"
- "Moreover" / "Furthermore" / "Additionally" (as paragraph openers)
- "Aims to explore" / "Seeks to provide"
- "We are committed to delivering..."
- "Our team of dedicated professionals..."
- "Take your X to the next level"
- "Game-changer" / "Best-in-class" / "World-class"
- "At the forefront of..."
- "Solutions that drive results"

## Punctuation Tells

**Em dashes — never use them.** AI reaches for em dashes constantly. Real humans use them occasionally. If you catch yourself typing one, that sentence probably needs to be two sentences.

**Semicolons in casual copy.** In marketing copy, web pages, and conversational prose, a semicolon almost always signals AI. Use "and," "but," or a period instead.

---

## Structural Patterns That Signal AI

**Paragraph uniformity.** AI paragraphs tend to be nearly the same length — four to five sentences each, stacked like identical bricks. A page where every paragraph is the same height wasn't written by a person who was thinking. Vary the lengths. Use a one-sentence paragraph. Let a thought breathe.

**The hedging reflex.** "Can potentially help you possibly improve..." — a string of qualifiers between the subject and the promise. One claim, stated plainly, beats three claims buried in modifiers. A real person says "I'll fix your website." Not "may be able to help potentially improve your online presence."

**The tricolon reflex.** AI loves groups of three: "creativity, innovation, and determination." Humans sometimes list two things. Sometimes four. Sometimes one.

**The summary conclusion.** AI almost always ends by restating what it just said. End with something that opens outward — a specific benefit, a question the reader is now asking, a call to action that feels earned, not tacked on.

**Symmetrical sections.** AI structures sections so every one has the same shape: headline, 3-sentence body, CTA. Real copy breathes differently. Some sections need more. Some need less. Trust the content.

## What Human Copy Does Instead

**It is specific.** Not "a trusted partner for your clients" but "when the interpreter doesn't show, the appointment has to be rescheduled and the settlement takes another month." Specificity is the single strongest trust signal. It proves someone was actually thinking.

**It names the real feeling.** Not "we understand your challenges" but "you've been burned by a no-show vendor before and you're not doing that again." Name the specific frustration.

**It makes one claim and means it.** The more hedges and qualifiers between a business and its promise, the less the reader trusts it. Pick the most important thing you do. Say it plainly. Trust it to land.

**It varies rhythm.** Short. Then a longer one that winds around a little before it lands. Then short again. Read it aloud. If every sentence lands with the same weight, rewrite.

---

## The Rhythm Test

Read the draft aloud. If every sentence arrives at the same pace and the same weight, something is wrong. Human writing breathes — it speeds up in urgency, slows down in credibility sections, stops short when something matters.

This passage has rhythm:
> We started in 2002 with one belief: language should never be a barrier to care. Twenty years later, we still show up. Every time.

This one doesn't:
> Since our founding in 2002, Between Worlds has been committed to delivering high-quality language services that help our clients achieve their goals in an effective and timely manner.

Same facts. One sounds like a person. The other sounds like a form.

## Web Copy Specific Notes

**Shorter is almost always better.** A hero section does not need four sentences of body copy. One strong sentence and a CTA is usually more effective than three medium ones.

**The first two sentences decide everything.** If a potential client lands on a service page, the first two sentences decide if they keep reading. A generic opening loses them. Name their situation — specifically — before you name your solution.

**The AI kill list applies to headlines most of all.** "Seamless Communication Solutions" is a headline generated by an AI that has never met a real client. "We Show Up. We Speak the Language." is a headline written by someone who knows what case managers are afraid of.

**SEO copy still needs to sound human.** Google's helpful content guidelines penalize content written for search engines instead of people. A page that sounds human performs better in search even if nobody reads every word. Don't sacrifice voice for keyword density.

## Cognitive Load (StoryBrand Principle)

Every word burns calories. If you make the reader work, they leave. This applies to website copy, emails, outreach, proposals, and anything a client or prospect reads.

**The grunt test.** Could someone glance at this for 5 seconds and know: (1) what you do, (2) how it makes their life better, (3) what to do next? If not, rewrite.

**One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has "and" connecting two separate thoughts, it's two sentences. The reader should never have to hold multiple ideas in their head to understand what you're saying.

**One CTA per section.** Every section should point toward one action. Two CTAs compete with each other and the reader picks neither.

**If they have to re-read it, cut it.** A sentence that requires a second pass is too complicated. Simplify or remove.

**Cut anything that doesn't move toward the action.** Background, qualifications, extra context, disclaimers. If it doesn't help the reader decide to take the next step, it's noise.

**Emails and outreach follow the same rules.** A follow-up email with four paragraphs of context before the ask will not get a reply. Lead with the point. One clear ask. Make it easy to say yes.

**Ask permission, don't announce.** "Could I go ahead and take care of that?" not "Let me take care of that." Especially with people who aren't paying you or who you're building trust with. Presumption kills warmth.

**Order items by commitment level.** Put the easy yes first, the softest commitment last. The reader says yes to the first item and carries that momentum through the list.

**"If you'd like" as a pressure valve.** Attach it to the item the person is least sure about. Gives them an easy out on one thing without rejecting the whole plan.

**Business blog posts (headleyweb.com) follow these rules too.** They get more room to breathe than a hero section, but every paragraph still needs to earn its place. If a section doesn't move the reader toward trusting you or taking action, cut it. This does NOT apply to editorial/narrative work (Southern Legends) which has its own voice guide.

## Don't Argue for Your Own Necessity

This applies to About pages, editorial intros, and anywhere you're writing in your own voice (not client StoryBrand copy).

**The pattern to avoid:** Opening with a gap statement that argues why the thing needs to exist. "Most people worth writing about never get written about" is a thesis disguised as an observation. It's StoryBrand's problem/solution shape showing up where it doesn't belong.

**What to do instead:** Start with what you do. Show the work. Let the reader figure out the gap. Don't announce the thesis — earn it by the end. A reader who arrives at the meaning alongside you trusts it more than one who was told up front.

**The test:** Does this opening sound like a pitch for why this project should exist? If yes, cut the argument and just start doing the thing.
